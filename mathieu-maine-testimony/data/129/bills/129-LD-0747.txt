2 Sec. 1.  15 MRSA §§394 and 395 are enacted to read:
3 §394.  Sale or transfer of firearms to prohibited persons; strict liability
4 1. Unlawful sale or transfer; strict liability.  A person may not sell or transfer a
5 firearm to a prohibited person as described in section 393.
6 2. Penalty.  The following penalties apply to violations of this section.
7 A.  A person who violates subsection 1 commits a civil violation for which a fine of 
8 $500 must be adjudged.
9 B.  A person who violates subsection 1 after having been adjudicated as having 
10 committed a civil violation under subsection 1 commits a Class E crime, which is a 
11 strict liability crime as defined in Title 17-A, section 34, subsection 4-A.
12 3. Affirmative defense.  It is an affirmative defense to prosecution under this section
13 that the seller or transferor of the firearm requested that a federally licensed firearms 
14 dealer complete a computerized background check under the Federal Bureau of 
15 Investigation, National Instant Criminal Background Check System, or successor 
16 background check system approved by the Attorney General of the United States, on the 
17 purchaser or transferee prior to the sale or transfer of the firearm and the background 
18 check indicated that the person was not a prohibited person.
19 §395.  Sale or transfer of firearms to prohibited persons; culpable mental state
20 1. Unlawful sale or transfer; culpable mental state.  A person is guilty of selling
21 or transferring a firearm to a prohibited person if that person intentionally or knowingly 
22 sells or transfers a firearm to a person that the seller or transferor knows or believes is 
23 prohibited from possessing a firearm under section 393.
24 2. Penalty.  A violation of this section is a Class D crime.

14 SUMMARY
15 This bill does the following.
16 1. It creates the civil violation of the sale or transfer of a firearm to a prohibited
17 person and provides that a 2nd or subsequent offense is a strict liability Class E crime.  
18 The bill also creates an affirmative defense to prosecution under this new civil violation 
19 and crime that the seller or transferor of the firearm requested a federally licensed 
20 firearms dealer to conduct a computerized background check under the Federal Bureau of 
21 Investigation, National Instant Criminal Background Check System on the purchaser or 
22 transferee prior to the sale or transfer of the firearm and the background check indicated 
23 that the purchaser or transferee was not a prohibited person.
24 2. It creates the Class D crime of the sale or transfer of a firearm to a prohibited
25 person.  A person is guilty of this crime if that person intentionally or knowingly sells or 
26 transfers a firearm to another person that the seller or transferor knows or believes is 
27 prohibited from possessing a firearm.
28 3. It increases the fine from $50 to $1,000 for the civil violation of giving a false or
29 fictitious name to a firearms dealer and makes the fine mandatory.
30 4. It requires the Chief of the State Police to convene a study group to review and
31 make recommendations regarding the so-called blue paper process as it relates to persons 
32 admitted to a psychiatric hospital on an emergency basis who are temporarily prohibited 
33 from possessing firearms.  Representatives from the following are invited to participate in 
34 this review:  the Office of the Attorney General, the Department of Health and Human 
35 Services, the Maine Prosecutors Association, Disability Rights Maine and any other 
36 interested parties that the Chief of the State Police determines appropriate.  The Chief of 
37 the State Police is required to report the recommendations of the study group to the Joint 
38 Standing Committee on Criminal Justice and Public Safety by January 30, 2020.  
39 Following receipt of the report, the Joint Standing Committee on Criminal Justice and 
40 Public Safety may report out a bill to the Second Regular Session of the 129th 
41 Legislature.
