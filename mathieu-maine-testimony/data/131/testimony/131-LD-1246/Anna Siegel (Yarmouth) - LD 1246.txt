LD 1246
Dear Senator Brenner, Representative Gramlich, and Honorable members of the 
Environment and Natural Resources committee,
My name is Anna Siegel and I am writing to urge you to vote “ought to pass” on LD 
1246. Humans have impacted the world to a degree where we have defined a geologic
era, the Anthropocene. A hallmark of the Anthropocene is the sixth mass extinction 
we are currently living in. This extinction is driven by habitat loss, habitat degradation
and encroachment, climate change, and ecological system failures. The resulting 
decline in biodiversity drives further ecosystem collapses and accelerates climate 
change, as biodiversity is key to carbon sequestration and other maintenance of 
natural processes. To halt this extinction and its consequences, we have to address the
habitat loss and alteration that is the main cause of species decline. LD 1246, in 
ensuring that endangered species habitat is considered “Significant Wildlife Habitat” 
and thus protected from development, is one solution to the habitat loss issue. 
It is particularly important to have a bill like this one in Maine, as our boreal forests 
are some of the last in the country and provide a home for declining lynx, black bear, 
moose, Northern Goshawks, and breeding warblers. Our brackish estuaries, rocky 
beaches, and sunlit plains host threatened grassland and shoreline species such as 
Upland Sandpiper and Piping Plover. These species are not only valuable for their 
roles in the ecosystem and their beauty. Tourists drive long distances to see moose, 
boreal birds, and other northern species around Moosehead Lake and Katahdin. 
Birders from all across the country go to Mount Desert Island, Rangeley, and Freeport
every year for our famous birding festivals. This ecotourism is important to the 
economy of rural towns who no longer have paper mills or other factories for jobs. 
Another endangered species that is important to both the environment and people are 
New England Cottontail rabbits. They have a prominent role in the food chain as both
herbivores that manage plants and prey that provide food for carnivores. Cottontail 
rabbits also protect communities and outdoor recreationalists from zoonotic diseases 
by eating the ticks common in their grassy habitat. The New England Cottontail is a 
state endangered species because of habitat loss. Their preferred habitat, shrublands 
and young forests, is now only 3% of the southern Maine landscape. LD 1246 would 
ensure that their habitat needs are considered when potential development is 
proposed.
LD 1246 is a key bill to protect Maine's wildlife, carbon sequestration systems, 
ecological processes, and conserve the species that drive ecotourism.
Respectfully submitted,
Anna Siegel
